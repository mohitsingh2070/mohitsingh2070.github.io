The Constitution of India: A Living Document Rooted in History and Shaped by Time

Constitution and Civilisation

The Constitution of India, often celebrated as the cornerstone of the world’s largest democracy, is more than a mere legal document. It embodies the aspirations, struggles, and ethos of a diverse nation. Just as an anecdotal story tells of a king whose life resided in a parrot, so too does the vitality of a democracy reside in its Constitution—a living indication of the rights we cherish, the freedoms we enjoy, and the voice we boldly raise. Yet, it is important to recognize the Constitution not as sacrosanct but as a historical document influenced by the politics and conditions of its time—a product of India’s journey rather than its ultimate destination.

The inclusion of "Bharat" in the opening line of the Indian Constitution—“India, that is Bharat, shall be a Union of States”—is a profound statement that intertwines the historical, cultural, and civilizational identity of the nation with its modern political framework. This dual nomenclature was a deliberate choice by the framers to reconcile the ancient heritage of Bharat, deeply rooted in the country's civilizational ethos, with the modern, constitutional identity of India as a sovereign democratic republic. Bharat signifies the timeless and indigenous identity of the land, drawing from texts like the Vishnu Purana and Mahabharata, while "India" reflects the nation’s engagement with the global community, a name that gained prominence during colonial rule. This pairing represents the continuum of India's journey—honoring its rich historical and cultural legacy while embracing its modern democratic aspirations. By enshrining "Bharat" alongside "India," the Constitution acknowledges the coexistence of these dual identities, ensuring that the spirit of the ancient civilization resonates within the framework of a contemporary nation-state.

The Constitution as a Reflection of Historical Necessities

The Constitution of India did not emerge in a vacuum; it was forged in the crucible of India’s socio-political struggles. Its roots trace back to colonial statutes such as the Government of India Acts of 1919 and 1935, as well as the Indian Independence Act of 1947. These documents laid the groundwork for the modern Constitution, providing a framework for governance and reflecting the political exigencies of British rule. As such, the Indian Constitution is deeply entwined with the historical context of its creation.

This historical lens is essential when analyzing the Constitution. It must be studied as a product of its time, influenced by the colonial legacy, political negotiations, and the aspirations of a newly independent nation. While the Constitution has been instrumental in shaping modern India, the country’s civilizational identity predates it by millennia. Bharat, as India is traditionally known, has always been a haven of diversity, offering refuge to persecuted communities such as Parsis and Jews long before any written Constitution. This intrinsic ethos of inclusivity and tolerance underscores that the Constitution, while crucial, is not the sole guardian of India’s unity or diversity.

Borrowed and Refined: The Origins of the Indian Constitution

The Indian Constitution is often labeled a "borrowed bag" because of its incorporation of provisions from various foreign constitutions. While this characterization holds some truth, it oversimplifies the deliberate and thoughtful process undertaken by the framers to adapt these features to India’s unique socio-political context. For instance, the Concurrent List and provisions for joint parliamentary sittings were drawn from Australia, while Canada inspired the federation with a strong center and residuary powers vested in it. From Ireland came the Directive Principles of State Policy and the method of electing the President, while Japan contributed the concept of the procedure established by law. The Soviet Union influenced the inclusion of Fundamental Duties and the ideals of justice, and the United Kingdom provided the framework for parliamentary government, bicameralism, and the rule of law. The United States contributed Fundamental Rights, judicial review, and impeachment processes, while Germany’s (Weimar) Constitution influenced provisions for suspending Fundamental Rights during emergencies. South Africa inspired procedures for constitutional amendments, and France added the ideals of liberty, equality, and fraternity. These borrowings, far from diminishing the originality of the Indian Constitution, reflect a pragmatic approach that tailored global best practices to India’s distinct challenges, resulting in a comprehensive and adaptable governance framework.

The Role of Dr. B.R. Ambedkar and the Drafting Process

While Dr. B.R. Ambedkar is often hailed as the architect of the Indian Constitution, this characterization overshadows the collective effort involved in its creation. Ambedkar served as the chairman of the drafting committee and played a pivotal role in fine-tuning the document. However, the Constitution was the result of extensive deliberations by the Constituent Assembly, which comprised 299 members (The Constituent Assembly of India had 389 members, but the number was reduced to 299 after the partition of India) representing diverse political and social perspectives.

The drafting process was an exhaustive exercise. The Assembly debated each article over 167 days (aound 11 sessions) spread across nearly three years, culminating in a document that balanced the aspirations of a nascent democracy with the realities of governance. Yet, it is worth noting that the majority of the Assembly members came from the majority community.

The Constitution Beyond Courts and Classrooms

Despite its foundational role, the Constitution remains largely inaccessible to the vast majority of Indians. With over 145,000 words, 395 articles, and 8 schedules, it is the longest written constitution in the world—an intimidating text that few outside the legal profession or academia have read in its entirety. This raises a critical question: Can the Constitution truly protect the rights and freedoms of a populace that remains largely unaware of its contents? I mean is the Constitution enforceable beyond the courtroom, especially in areas far removed from Delhi, where an overwhelming majority—perhaps 99%, to be optimistic—have never read its provisions?

The reach of the Constitution extends far beyond the courts and classrooms. It is a living document that governs the actions of the judiciary, executive, legislature, and bureaucracy. Yet, in a healthy society, governance is equally guided by morality and ethics—values that are not explicitly codified but are integral to the nation’s civilizational ethos. Bharat’s democratic and civilized character is rooted in its ancient concept of Dharma, a term conspicuously absent from the Constitution.

The Constitution as a Living Document

The Indian Constitution has been amended 106 times as of this writing, reflecting its dynamic and adaptive nature. This adaptability underscores its status as a "living document," capable of evolving to meet the changing needs of society. One of the most profound judicial interpretations of the Constitution came in the Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala case (1973), where a 13-judge bench established the "basic structure doctrine," asserting that certain fundamental principles of the Constitution cannot be amended.

Ambedkar himself acknowledged the limitations of the Constitution, famously stating:

"However good a Constitution may be, if those who are implementing it are not good, it will prove to be bad. However bad a Constitution may be, if those implementing it are good, it will prove to be good."

This statement highlights the primacy of human agency over constitutional provisions. It is ultimately the people and their leaders who determine the effectiveness of governance.

The Resilience of the Constitution

India’s darkest constitutional moment came during the Emergency (1975–77), when civil liberties were suspended, and democratic institutions were undermined. Yet, this period also demonstrated the resilience of the Constitution. Within a few years, the democratic process was restored, and the Constitution emerged stronger, proving its capacity to withstand even the gravest challenges.

Conclusion

The Constitution of India is a testament to the nation’s journey through history—a reflection of its struggles, aspirations, and compromises. While it is not without flaws, it remains a vital instrument of governance and a symbol of the democratic values that Bharat has long cherished. However, the Constitution is not a panacea, it is merely a tool, and its efficacy depends on the integrity and competence of those who wield it.

Ultimately, the strength of India’s democracy lies not in the Constitution alone but in the civilizational ethos of Bharat, which has upheld values of tolerance, inclusivity, and resilience for centuries. As we celebrate and critique the Constitution, we must do so with an understanding of its historical context and an unwavering commitment to the principles of Dharma that continue to guide this ancient land.
