A Whispered Truth Between Life and Death

Silence, Death, and the Unavoidable Truth

It isn’t easy to be silent. Yet, in silence, the secrets of living and dying quietly reveal themselves.

Silence is an art we are never taught in schools, the art of entering, discovering, and abiding in stillness, especially at the time of death. If we truly learned this art, life itself would appear differently.

Death is simply the end of biological activity. It can arrive at any moment, untimely, unexpected, indifferent to success or failure. At the height of joy, the peak of achievement, or in the prime of life, death makes no distinction. Even a natural death carries a sorrow that cannot be rushed, only lived through.

The person we once referred to by name becomes only a body. Achievements, honours, memories all fall away. What disappears is the spark we call life. Who has gone? Where do they go? We rarely ask. We avoid the topic as if death were unnatural, when in truth it is the most natural event we will ever encounter. It happens to all, and from nature’s perspective, it is necessary.

When we witness a body decay, we instinctively turn away, covering our noses. Yet that same fate awaits every one of us. This truth is harsh only for those who refuse to accept it.

The body burns, dissolves, returns to nothing. A life that once crawled into being fades back into emptiness. Surely we are not born merely to die. There must be meaning. We seek it like the six blind men describing an elephant, each grasping a part, none seeing the whole. Life and death resist definition. The moment we try to capture them too tightly, they slip away.

Death as Renewal, Life as Process

To understand anything deeply, we must let go of what we already think we know. Letting go is a kind of death, and without it, nothing new can be born. Death renews life. It is how learning truly happens.

Science and evolution offer explanations, yet your very birth remains a mystery. Every today carries the death of yesterday. Childhood had to die for adolescence to appear. Adolescence died for adulthood. This movement continues endlessly. Before birth, you existed as potential in your father. Before that, further back still, ultimately in the beginnings of the universe.

Instead of mourning impermanence, we could see ourselves as continually giving way to new creation. The plants we nurture, the people we meet, the trees we admire, everything is in the process of becoming. Leaves turn green, then yellow, red, brown, and finally fall. Winter strips a tree bare, yet even that barrenness holds a stark beauty.

Death is not a noun but a verb, a process. Every name we give is only a temporary label for something that is always changing, evolving, regenerating.

If I am a leaf, I have no date of birth or date of death. I am eaten by insects. I decay into soil. I become nourishment for new life. I live and die moment by moment. Why would a leaf fear death? It meets death only when it arrives. Death does not occupy its mind, so it truly lives, simply for the joy of living.

We, however, crave permanence. We reject change, resist death, and in doing so, resist life.

Only Because It Ends

Pain is the only unifying emotion,
which strips us bare and makes us honest.
joy deceives, pride blinds, but pain reveals.
Every smile carries the shadow of its end, every heartbeat ticks toward silence.
to live is to walk willingly toward your executioner.

And yet, knowing this, there is a strange freedom.
when nothing can be held, you stop clinging.
when death is certain, life becomes sharp, urgent, unbearably precious.
The reason for life is death.
only against the certainty of the end does the beginning have meaning.

Letting Go and Rediscovering Life

When we see people dying, we turn to what society and media reflect back to us. Even then, we cling to names and identities. Many do not die peacefully. Many do not even live peacefully. We worry ourselves to death without ever tasting peace.

Let us do a small exercise.

Make a fist. Hold it tight. Now release it.

That simple act of letting go brings an unexpected freedom. Let go of your wants, your name, your family, your concerns, even your breath, and ultimately yourself. What remains is a cleansed hand, capable of holding both life and death. In every ending lies renewal.

Life must be rediscovered moment by moment. It cannot be assumed or possessed.

Ask a child how life is going. A child will not answer. They are too busy living. The moment you say you “know” life, you have already stopped living it. Three meals, clothing, shelter, sex, work, entertainment, repetitive thinking, this is not life. It is merely resistance to existence.

Life must be discovered. And to discover it, you must set aside everything you have collected, beliefs, customs, memories, ideas. Until you release what you carry from the past, you will never meet life as it is.

A child wants to explore. He wants to walk, not learn it from an app. He has no manual, yet he knows. As he grows into adulthood, he becomes like us, and eventually like an eighty year old retired person. What are we losing along the way? We are losing life itself.

Yet we have the ability to become young again. We can shed old habits and dysfunction, and nurture attitudes that make us glow. It is our life, and no one can live it for us. We are all going from dust to dust. The difference lies only in the details.

How you die depends entirely on how you live.
